The Business Problem

Why Netflix Obsesses Over Thumbnails

Netflix has over 300 million subscribers worldwide. Every time one of them opens the app, they are confronted with dozens of shows and movies competing for their attention. The window to grab someone is brutally short — Netflix's own research suggests a viewer decides what to watch within roughly 90 seconds of browsing, or they leave.

The artwork thumbnail — that small rectangular image representing a title — is the single most powerful variable Netflix can control in that window. The same show can have a dramatically different click-through rate depending on whether the thumbnail shows a character's face, a dramatic scene, a moody atmosphere, or an action moment. Which one wins is not a matter of opinion. It is a matter of data.

This is where A/B testing becomes a core business tool. Rather than letting designers or executives guess which artwork is better, Netflix runs controlled experiments: show Artwork A to one group of users, Artwork B to another group, measure who clicks more, and let the numbers decide. This project replicates that process in Python.

The Method

What Is A/B Testing and Why Does It Matter for AI?

A/B testing is one of the most important tools in the modern data scientist and business analyst's toolkit. At its core, it is a controlled experiment: you take two versions of something — a thumbnail, a price, a product page, a recommendation algorithm — and you expose different groups of real users to each version. Then you measure the outcome you care about.

In machine learning and AI systems, A/B testing is how companies validate that a new model or algorithm is actually better than the old one before rolling it out to everyone. Netflix does not just replace one recommendation engine with another because a data scientist thinks the new one is better. They test both on real users, measure the results, and only deploy the winner if the evidence is statistically convincing. This discipline is what separates companies that make data-driven decisions from companies that make opinion-driven ones.

The key metrics in a click-focused experiment are simple: Click-Through Rate (CTR) is the percentage of people who see something and then click on it. Higher CTR means the artwork is doing its job. The experiment here tests whether two different CTRs — 24% for Artwork A and 30% for Artwork B — represent a real, trustworthy difference, or just a lucky streak of random variation.

The Project

What I Built in Three Steps

01

Simulated 1,000 Users in a Controlled Experiment

Using Python and NumPy, I randomly assigned 1,000 simulated users to one of two groups — Group A or Group B — with a 50/50 split. Each user in Group A had a 24% probability of clicking the artwork; each user in Group B had a 30% probability. Whether each user actually clicked was determined by a random draw, mimicking the unpredictable nature of real human behavior. Results were stored in a structured pandas DataFrame with three columns: User ID, Artwork Version, and Clicked.

02

Calculated CTR and Visualized the Results

I grouped the data by artwork version and calculated three summary statistics for each: total users in the group, total clicks recorded, and the click-through rate as a percentage. I then built a clean bar chart using matplotlib to visually compare the two CTR values side by side, along with a lift calculation showing how much better Artwork B performed relative to Artwork A.

03

Ran a Statistical Significance Test

Observing that Artwork B had a higher CTR is not enough on its own — the difference could be random noise. I ran a Two-Proportion Z-Test using Python's statsmodels library to calculate a p-value: the probability that the observed gap between the two groups could have occurred by chance alone. A p-value below 0.05 is the industry-standard threshold for declaring a result statistically significant and trustworthy enough to act on.

The Code

Complete Python Implementation

The following is the complete, annotated code used to run this experiment — from simulation through statistical testing. This runs as a single block in Google Colab or any Python environment with NumPy, pandas, matplotlib, scipy, and statsmodels installed.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest

# Set seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# --- Simulation Parameters ---
n_users = 1000
prob_A  = 0.24   # Click probability for Artwork A
prob_B  = 0.30   # Click probability for Artwork B

# --- Step 1: Assign users randomly to Group A or Group B ---
user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])

# --- Step 2: Simulate clicks based on group assignment ---
clicks = []
for assignment in assignments:
    if assignment == 'A':
        click = np.random.binomial(1, prob_A)
    else:
        click = np.random.binomial(1, prob_B)
    clicks.append(click)

# --- Step 3: Build the DataFrame ---
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id': user_ids,
    'artwork': assignments,
    'clicked': clicks
})

# --- Step 4: Summary Statistics ---
summary = df.groupby('artwork')['clicked'].agg(
    total_users='count',
    total_clicks='sum',
    ctr=lambda x: round(x.mean() * 100, 2)
).reset_index()

print("=== A/B Test Summary ===")
print(summary.to_string(index=False))

# --- Step 5: Bar Chart ---
colors = ['#E50914', '#ff6b6b']

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
bars = ax.bar(summary['artwork'], summary['ctr'],
             color=colors, width=0.4,
             edgecolor='white', linewidth=1.2)

for bar, ctr in zip(bars, summary['ctr']):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
           bar.get_height() + 0.4, f'{ctr}%',
           ha='center', va='bottom',
           fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')

ax.set_title('A/B Test: Click-Through Rate by Artwork Version',
            fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', pad=15)
ax.set_xlabel('Artwork Version', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylabel('Click-Through Rate (%)', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylim(0, max(summary['ctr']) + 8)
ax.yaxis.grid(True, linestyle='--', alpha=0.7)
ax.spines['top'].set_visible(False)
ax.spines['right'].set_visible(False)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# --- Step 6: Lift Calculation ---
ctr_A = summary.loc[summary['artwork'] == 'A', 'ctr'].values[0]
ctr_B = summary.loc[summary['artwork'] == 'B', 'ctr'].values[0]
lift  = round(((ctr_B - ctr_A) / ctr_A) * 100, 2)

print(f"\n=== Performance Lift ===")
print(f"Artwork A CTR  : {ctr_A}%")
print(f"Artwork B CTR  : {ctr_B}%")
print(f"Lift (B over A): +{lift}%")

# --- Step 7: Two-Proportion Z-Test ---
group_A  = df[df['artwork'] == 'A']
group_B  = df[df['artwork'] == 'B']

clicks_A = group_A['clicked'].sum()
clicks_B = group_B['clicked'].sum()
n_A      = len(group_A)
n_B      = len(group_B)

count    = np.array([clicks_B, clicks_A])
nobs     = np.array([n_B, n_A])

z_stat, p_value = proportions_ztest(count, nobs)

print(f"\n=== Statistical Significance Test ===")
print(f"Z-Statistic : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-Value     : {p_value:.4f}")

alpha = 0.05
if p_value < alpha:
    print("✅ STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT — roll out Artwork B.")
else:
    print("❌ NOT significant — run the experiment longer.")

The Two-Proportion Z-Test returned a p-value well below the 0.05 threshold. This means there is less than a 5% probability that the observed gap between Artwork A's 24% CTR and Artwork B's 30% CTR occurred by random chance. The data provides strong, statistically defensible evidence that Artwork B is the genuinely better-performing option.

To put the lift in plain terms: for every 1,000 users who see the artwork, Artwork B generates approximately 60 more clicks than Artwork A. At Netflix's scale — hundreds of millions of users browsing daily across thousands of titles — this kind of difference compounds into an enormous impact on watch hours, subscriber satisfaction, and ultimately retention and revenue.

Business Recommendation

What Netflix Should Do

Deploy Artwork B as the Default Thumbnail

The experiment produced a clear, statistically significant winner. Artwork B outperformed Artwork A by approximately 6 percentage points in click-through rate, representing a relative lift of roughly 25%. The p-value confirms this difference is not a statistical accident. The recommendation is to retire Artwork A and roll Artwork B out as the default thumbnail for this title across the full user base.

Beyond this single experiment, there are two additional steps a real Netflix data team would take. First, they would consider personalization: rather than one winner for all users, they would analyze whether specific audience segments — action fans, romance viewers, subscribers who favor a particular actor — respond differently to different artwork variants, and serve each segment the thumbnail most likely to resonate with them personally.

Second, they would treat this experiment as one data point in a continuous cycle. Artwork B wins today, but viewer tastes shift, new seasons launch, and cultural moments change what resonates. Netflix runs hundreds of these experiments simultaneously, constantly refreshing what users see based on what the data says is working right now. The experiment is never truly over — it is just in a new phase.

The broader takeaway for any business considering AI-driven experimentation: the value is not in any single test. It is in building the organizational discipline to run tests continuously, trust the results, and ship the winner — every time.
